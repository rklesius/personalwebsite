About Me

I am a student at the University of South Florida, graduating in May of 2019 with a Bachelor of Science in Computer Science and a minor in Astronomy. I am the Vice President of Internal Affairs for Women in Computer Science and Engineering (WICSE) and the Treasurer of the SCUBA club at USF.

During the summer of 2018, I completed an internship with JP Morgan & Chase where I did full stack development over a 10 week period. Since then, I have learned much about what it means to be a full stack developer, including honing my web development skills while continuing to improve my knowledge of algorithms and data structures. I am fluent in Java, C, C++, Python, HTML, CSS, Javascript, and Arduino. I have also been exposed to Ruby, SQL and CQL so it would be easy for me to pick those up.

I am passionate about inspiring the next generation of programmers and presenting to kids about how important programming is in today's world as part of WICSE. Another one of my passions is to inspire other women by showing them the importance of going out of your comfort zone (For example, leading luncheons and workshops for WICSE). Outside of programming, I also love SCUBA diving: nothing clears your head and relaxes you more than being in the underwater world.

Personal Philosophy

"Going out of your comfort zone is the hardest thing to do and the most effective way to improve"

I was part of a generation that grew up in love with computers: from a young age I regarded programmers as modern-day wizards. Perhaps I was a little naïve when I decided that my major was going to be Computer Science. At the time programming was as foreign as Klingon and as daunting as brain surgery.

My first year of college was mostly prerequisites, but I took an elective called "Intro to Programming" which taught Python. To this day I will never forget sitting in my dorm room and programming for the first time: those famous words "Hello World" forever changed my life-I was hooked.

From there I felt intimidated, I realized I had to get a job at the end of all this. The professional world was foreign and terrifying. Being a good programmer wasn't enough; I had to be all those buzzwords that companies like to hear. Words like "leadership" and "experience". At the time I was content with putting my head down and doing the schoolwork, while hoping that an opportunity would will itself into existence.

After two semesters of plateauing, I got an email from WICSE. It was about a hackathon that JP Morgan & Chase (JPMC) was hosting, and I decided to sign up. I had never been to a hackathon before: it was intimidating. However, I decided I would go and learn from the experience, even though it was daunting. Despite my fears, my performance at that hackathon prompted JPMC to offer me a summer internship, and I was ecstatic.

Over winter break, I got a text from a friend saying that WICSE was looking for a new marketing chair due to the previous chair graduating. I had absolutely zero experience running a club, but I agreed to do it. Next thing I know, I'm giving an Arduino workshop, which became the highest attended meeting of the semester- banishing my fear of public speaking. At the end of that semester, I was elected Vice President of Internal Affairs of WICSE for the 2018-2019 school year.

My internship began the following summer, and it was a terrifyingly incredible experience as my first step into the industry. My project for those 10 weeks depended on web development and databases, which at the time I had zero experience in. In the end, I finished the project-it was a resounding success. Which is why, it was so crushing that JPMC did not extend an offer.

Even though my personal philosophy, “Going out of your comfort zone is the hardest thing to do and the most effective way to improve”, seemed to falter at the time, it held true. I walked away with a new set of skills, not only pertaining to programming but also to professional development. And, I had the following semester to look forward to! In that semester, I became even more motivated in WICSE with my promotion: I kickstarted the WICSE Mentorship program to help incoming freshman, I gave computer science presentations to local middle schoolers and high schoolers, I gave two Arduino Workshops, and I pursued personal projects and tackled larger projects in academia. Even in SCUBA Club, I was diving out of my comfort zone by leading dives. Looking back, if I had gotten an offer from JPMC, I probably would not have improved as much as I had this last semester, and I probably would have plateaued again. Even though the improvement was not immediate, my personal philosophy still held.

My personal philosophy defines all of my success. From that naïve freshman programming in Python to the confident programmer I am today, I have been shaped by going out of my comfort zone. Defying my fears, I have pursued my goals. My success is from going beyond what I think I can do, driving myself to improve along the way. I can confidently say that, “To be bold, to dive deeper, to always strive for success” that is who I am.
