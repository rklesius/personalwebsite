About Me

I am a student at the University of South Florida, graduating in May of 2019 with a degree in Computer Science and a minor in Astronomy. I am the Vice President of Internal Affairs for Women in Computer Science and Engineering and the Treasurer of the SCUBA club at USF.

During the summer of 2018, I had an internship with JP Morgan & Chase where I did full stack development over a 10 week period. Since then, I have learned much about what it means to be a full stack developer, including honing my web development skills while continuing to improve my knowledge of algorithms and data structures. I am fluent in Java, C, C++, HTML, CSS, Javascript, and Arduino. I have also been exposed to Python, Ruby, SQL and CQL.

Personal Philosophy

Going out of your comfort zone is the hardest thing to do and the most effective way to improve.

When decided that my major was going to be Computer Science, I had never programmed a day in my life. Perhaps I was a little naïve, but I was part of the generation that grew up in love with computers. Technology has always amazed me, even from a young age I thought that programmers were the modern day equivalent of wizards.

My first year of college was mostly prerequisites, but I took an elective called "Intro to Programming" which taught Python. To this day I will never forget sitting in my dorm room and programming for the first time; those famous words of "Hello World" forever changed my life. I was hooked.

From there I began to get intimidated, I realized I had to get a job at the end of all this. I was no longer going to be judged on hard work and merit but I also had to fight to show people that I not only was a good programmer, but I was also all those buzzwords that companies like to hear. Words like "leadership" and "experience". The professional world was foreign and terrifying. For a good year I was content with putting my head down and doing the schoolwork, hoping that an opportunity would will itself into existence.

I got an email in the October following two semesters of plateauing, from a club I was in called Women in Computer Science and Engineering (WICSE). It was about a hackathon that JP Morgan & Chase was hosting and I decided to sign up. I had never been to a hackathon before and I was really intimidated by the idea, but I thought that I would just go and try to have a good time even if I didn't do well in the hackathon. After that hackathon I got an offer from JP Morgan & Chase for a summer internship and I was ecstatic.

Over winter break after accepting my internship from JPMC, I got a text from a friend saying that WICSE was looking for a new marketing chair since the previous one graduated. I had absolutely zero experience running a club, but I agreed to do it. Next thing I know I'm giving an Arduino workshop and it becomes the highest attended meeting of the semester; with a fear of public speaking banished. At the end of that Spring semester, I was elected Vice President of Internal Affairs for the 2018-2019 school year.

My internship began in the summer of that year, and it was a terrifyingly incredible experience and my first in the industry. At the time, I had zero experience with web development and databases, but my project for those 10 weeks depended almost entirely on those things. In the end, I finished the project and it was a resounding success, and that is why it was so crushing that I didn't get an offer.

For once, going out of my comfort zone and my personal philosophy was wrong. Still though, I had a lot to look forward to in the following fall semester. In that semester I got even more motivated in WICSE with my promotion. I kickstarted the WICSE Mentorship program to help incoming freshman, I gave presentations to local Middle Schoolers and High Schoolers to introduce them to computer science, I gave two more Arduino Workshops, I pursued more personal projects and tackled larger projects in academia. Even in SCUBA Club I was diving out of my comfort zone by leading dives. Looking back, if I had gotten an offer from JPMC, I probably would not have improved as much as I did this last semester, and I probably would have plateaued again. Even though the improvement was not immediate, my personal philosophy still held.

I hold true to my personal philosophy because it totally defines all success I have. From that naïve freshman programming in Python in her dorm room to who I am now years later, I give my success from stretching the limit of what I can do, and who I am. To be bold, to dive deeper, to be always striving for success, that is who I am now.
